A Pipeline to Explore and Investigate Multidimensional Media Space using Multimodal LLMs

How can researchers explore and study the complex real-world digital media experiences at scale?

To answer this question, we developed the Media Content Atlas (MCA)‚Äîa customizable AI-powered pipeline that augments inductive inquiry through content-based clustering, topic modeling, image retrieval, and interactive visualization. Powered by open-source multimodal models (e.g., CLIP, LLaVA-One Vision), MCA eliminates the need for manual annotation while safeguarding sensitive data. By transforming raw screen data into meaningful structures, MCA uncovers patterns in digital media behavior that were previously impossible to study at this scale.

Background: The Problem

People spend over eight hours a day on screens, making digital media a central part of our lives. Yet, most research still relies on traditional screen-use metrics‚Äîsuch as time spent per app‚Äîwhich fail to capture:

  • üì± What users actually see: an Instagram screen about makeup tutorials is classified the same as one involving cyberbullying or harassment‚Äîeven though their psychological and social impacts are vastly different.
  • üîÑ How content flows across apps: Switching between platforms (e.g., reading news, then watching a related video) is not captured.
  • üìä Thematic insights: Studies rely on app categories, which are rigid and often irrelevant to user experiences.

To address this, continuous screen data collection has emerged, capturing everything displayed on users' screens. However, media experiences are infinitely describable‚Äîthe same dataset can hold vastly different meanings depending on the researcher‚Äôs perspective and research questions.

Despite this richness, there are no scalable tools that allow researchers to interact with, explore, and analyze these vast digital experiences. Working with such massive, sensitive, and unstructured multimodal data remains an overwhelming challenge.

Data: Capturing Media Content Beyond App Names

To design and evaluate Media Content Atlas, we used 1.12 million screens (10,000 screenshots per participant) collected from 112 participants over the course of one month. This data comes from the Human Screenome Project, where a study-specific app captured a screenshot every 5 seconds whenever the screen was on, creating a continuous, moment-by-moment record of digital experiences.

Manually reviewing this dataset would be nearly impossible‚Äîa researcher would need to spend over 190 continuous 8-hour workdays just to view each screen for 5 seconds. The sheer scale and complexity of this data make AI-augmented exploration and analysis essential.

Expert Evaluation & Findings

To evaluate MCA‚Äôs clustering, topic labeling and retrieval capabilities, we conducted an assessment with four domain experts specializing in media processes and effects across fields (eg., communication, psychology, medicine, education). Through detailed think-aloud sessions and structured ratings (three hours per expert), they provided feedback on and evaluated:

  • Cluster Relevance: 96% of the topic labels generated for the clusters were rated as relevant or highly relevant.
  • Description Accuracy: 83% of AI-generated content descriptions were considered accurate or highly accurate.
    • Content Similarity: 89% of images within clusters were deemed similar or highly similar, confirming MCA's effectiveness in grouping related content.
  • Image Retrieval Performance: 79.5% of retrieved images were rated relevant or highly relevant to search queries.
  • Comparison with Traditional Groupings: Experts unanimously rated MCA as more informative, descriptive, and useful for their research compared to traditional app-based and app-category-based groupings.

Key Takeaways & Future Work

As digital media shapes our behaviors, culture, and decisions, capturing and studying these experiences is more critical than ever. This study introduced the Media Content Atlas (MCA)‚Äîa first-of-its-kind pipeline for studying large-scale, high-complexity screen data using multimodal LLMs.

MCA moves beyond traditional screen-use metrics, enabling researchers to:

  • üìä Analyze moment-by-moment screen content
  • üîç Cluster media based on meaning
  • üìë Conduct topic modeling and labeling
  • üñºÔ∏è Retrieve images from theoretical queries
  • üéõÔ∏è Explore multi-perspective visualizations

Unlike traditional approaches, MCA requires no manual annotation while safeguarding sensitive data, allowing researchers to study digital media at scale efficiently and securely.

Our Next Steps: Future work will use semi-supervised validation methods, where researchers refine AI-generated clusters. Additional modular views (e.g., outcome-based, longitudinal) will further enhance MCA‚Äôs flexibility across research contexts.
